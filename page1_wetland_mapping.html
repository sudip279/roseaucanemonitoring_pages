The Problem

Why Mapping Roseau Cane is Scientifically Challenging

Coastal wetlands are spectrally complex environments where Roseau cane co-occurs with other marsh vegetation — making separation through conventional vegetation indices unreliable at landscape scale.

01
Spectral Mixing with Co-occurring Species
Standard NDVI-based assessments cannot distinguish Roseau cane from adjacent marsh grasses, cattails, and shrubs. This leads to overestimation of die-back extent and inaccurate spatial attribution.
02
Limited Large-Scale Ground Sampling
Coastal wetland accessibility is severely restricted by shallow water, soft substrate, and dense vegetation — making traditional transect-based validation impractical at the delta scale.
03
Dynamic Water Level Interference
Tidal and riverine water fluctuations alter reflectance signatures seasonally, creating temporal inconsistencies that complicate multi-year change detection using single-date imagery.
04
Fragmented Spatial Distribution
Roseau cane exists as isolated patches and continuous stands across a 200+ km² landscape, requiring methods capable of capturing both sparse and dense distribution patterns simultaneously.
Methodology

Classification Workflow

01
Landsat
Satellite Image Acquisition
Cloud-free Landsat-8 and Sentinel-2 imagery acquired for 2010, 2019, and 2022 growing seasons. Images selected for minimum water-level difference to reduce tidal interference.
02
Field + Drone
Training Sample Collection
Field surveys and drone-derived high-resolution imagery used to generate spectrally pure training polygons for each land cover class, overcoming ground accessibility limitations.
03
Classification
Supervised Classification & Extraction
Machine learning classifier applied to separate Roseau cane from other wetland vegetation types. Results cross-validated against NAIP aerial photography and Google Earth imagery.
04
Sentinel-2
Accuracy Assessment & Refinement
Comprehensive accuracy assessment with confusion matrix. Classified patterns compared against field observations to ensure the maps accurately reflect ground conditions.
Remote Sensing Data

Dual-Sensor Approach: Landsat & Sentinel-2

Using two complementary satellite sensors provides temporal depth and spatial detail — critical for separating closely related wetland vegetation at landscape scale.

🛰
Historical Archive
Landsat 8/9
Provides a multi-decade archive enabling change detection back to 2010. Used to establish baseline Roseau cane distribution and track long-term die-back trends in the lower MRD.
  • Spatial resolution 30 m
  • Revisit time 16 days
  • Bands used Visible, NIR, SWIR
  • Years analyzed 2010, 2019, 2022
📡
High Resolution
Sentinel-2 MSI
Finer spatial resolution (10 m) and red-edge bands provide improved discrimination between Roseau cane and co-occurring marsh species at the patch level — newly incorporated into the 2022 classification.
  • Spatial resolution 10 m (VIS/NIR)
  • Revisit time 5 days
  • Bands used Red-edge, NIR, SWIR
  • Years analyzed 2022 (new)

Research Significance

Why This Mapping Matters

🌿
Species-Level Discrimination
First large-scale map to separate Roseau cane from other marsh vegetation across the entire lower MRD, enabling species-specific monitoring rather than generic wetland assessment.
📊
Decision Support for Restoration
Spatial distribution data and change patterns across three decades provide actionable intelligence for wetland restoration prioritization and Roseau cane conservation strategy.
🔬
Hypothesis Testing at Scale
Large-scale mapped distributions allow statistical testing of field-observed hypotheses about Roseau cane growth preferences relative to river distance, hydrology, and soil conditions.
